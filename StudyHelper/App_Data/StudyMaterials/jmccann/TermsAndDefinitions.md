# Terms and Definitions

A graduate-level glossary of accounting terms compiled from Module 1 (Introduction to Financial Accounting & the Financial Statements), Module 2 (Internal Controls, Cash, Ethics & Financial Statement Analysis), and Module 3 (Accrual Accounting & Accounts Receivable), supplemented with standard accounting knowledge.

---

## Foundational Concepts

Accounting: The process by which a company's diverse economic activities are translated into a set of objective numerical measures that communicate information about the firm's performance, problems, and prospects to internal and external users.

Financial Accounting: The branch of accounting concerned with the preparation of general-purpose financial statements for external users such as investors, creditors, and regulators, in conformity with established reporting standards (e.g., GAAP).

The Accounting Equation: The fundamental identity underlying double-entry bookkeeping: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Assets represent investing activities (uses of funds); liabilities represent non-owner financing and equity represents owner financing (sources of funds).

Economic Entity Assumption: The principle that the financial activities of a business are kept separate and distinct from the personal financial activities of its owners and from other business entities.

Going Concern Assumption: The presumption that a reporting entity will continue to operate for the foreseeable future and is not in danger of liquidation, justifying the deferral of certain expenses and the use of historical cost.

Historical Cost Concept: The measurement principle under which most assets are reported on the balance sheet at the amount originally paid to acquire them, regardless of subsequent increases in market value.

Book Value vs. Market Value: Book value is the carrying amount of an asset or equity on the balance sheet (often based on historical cost less accumulated depreciation), while market value reflects what the asset or equity would currently command in an active market; the two frequently diverge.

Economics of Accounting Information: The idea that external financial reporting facilitates economic transactions and the efficient allocation of resources by reducing information asymmetry; investors demand it to lower uncertainty, and companies supply it to reduce their cost of capital.

## Standard-Setting and Regulatory Bodies

Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB): The independent, non-governmental body responsible for promulgating Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) in the United States through an open, due-process standard-setting framework.

Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP): The body of accounting standards, conventions, and rules that companies must follow when preparing financial statements in the United States.

International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS): The set of global accounting standards issued by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB), used by most countries outside the United States.

Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC): The U.S. federal agency that regulates companies whose stock is traded on a U.S. exchange; its mission is to ensure that investors receive full and fair information about publicly traded companies. The SEC requires periodic filings such as the 10-K, 10-Q, and 8-K.

Form 10-K: The comprehensive annual report that publicly traded U.S. companies must file with the SEC, containing audited financial statements, MD&A, and detailed business disclosures.

Form 10-Q: The quarterly report filed by publicly traded U.S. companies with the SEC, containing unaudited financial statements and updates on operations.

Form 8-K: A current report filed with the SEC to disclose significant corporate events that shareholders should know about between periodic filings (e.g., mergers, executive changes).

Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) of 2002: U.S. federal legislation enacted in response to major accounting scandals. Section 404 places responsibility on management for establishing and maintaining a sound internal control structure over financial reporting and requires public companies to annually assess and report on the effectiveness of those controls.

Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB): A nonprofit corporation established by SOX to oversee the audits of public companies and broker-dealers in order to protect investors and the public interest.

## The Financial Statements

Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position): A financial statement that reports a company's resources (assets) and the claims against those resources (liabilities and equity) at a single point in time. It mirrors the accounting equation and is a "snapshot" of the business.

Income Statement (Statement of Operations): A financial statement that reports the performance of the firm over a period of time. Its key elements are revenues, expenses, gains, and losses.

Statement of Cash Flows: A financial statement reporting cash inflows and outflows during a period, classified into operating, investing, and financing activities.

Statement of Changes in Stockholders' Equity: A statement that reconciles beginning and ending balances of each component of stockholders' equity (common stock, additional paid-in capital, retained earnings, etc.) during the reporting period.

Notes to the Financial Statements (Footnotes): Disclosures containing information about assumptions, estimates, measurement procedures, accounting policies, and details behind the summary numbers reported in the body of the financial statements.

Management's Discussion and Analysis (MD&A): A narrative section of the annual report in which management explains the company's financial condition, results of operations, liquidity, and known risks and uncertainties.

Independent Auditor's Report: The opinion issued by an external auditor regarding whether the financial statements are presented fairly, in all material respects, in conformity with GAAP.

Audit Opinion: A formal statement issued by the independent auditor; it provides reasonable (not absolute) assurance that the statements are free of material misstatement based on a sampling of transactions, not an examination of every transaction.

## Income Statement Elements

Revenues: Inflows of net assets during a period from delivering or producing goods, rendering services, or other activities that constitute the entity's ongoing major or central operations.

Expenses: Outflows or using up of assets, or incurrence of liabilities, during a period from delivering goods, rendering services, or carrying out other activities that constitute the entity's ongoing major or central operations.

Gains: Increases in equity (net assets) from peripheral or incidental transactions of an entity, distinct from revenues earned from primary operations.

Losses: Decreases in equity (net assets) from peripheral or incidental transactions of an entity, distinct from expenses incurred in primary operations.

Net Income: The bottom-line measure of overall performance, computed as Revenues – Expenses + Gains – Losses.

Gross Profit: Net Sales minus Cost of Goods Sold; measures the profitability of a company's core merchandising or production activities before considering operating overhead.

Operating Income: Net Sales – Cost of Goods Sold – Operating Expenses; reflects the operating performance of the entity exclusive of financing and investing activities and taxes.

Operating Expenses: The usual and customary costs incurred to support a company's main business activities (e.g., cost of goods sold, selling expenses, depreciation, research and development).

Non-Operating Items: Income statement items related to a company's financing and investing activities rather than its primary operations (e.g., interest expense, interest or dividend income, gains and losses from the sale of securities).

Earnings Per Share (EPS): The amount of net income associated with each share of common stock, computed on an equivalent share basis. It is a key indicator of profitability on a per-share basis.

Basic EPS: EPS computed using actual weighted-average common shares outstanding during the period (historical amounts).

Diluted EPS: EPS computed assuming the exercise or conversion of all dilutive potential common shares (stock options, convertible securities), intended to present a worst-case scenario for current shareholders.

Limitations of the Income Statement: Reported income (a) omits items that cannot be measured reliably (e.g., brand value), (b) is affected by the accounting methods chosen (e.g., straight-line vs. accelerated depreciation), and (c) involves judgment in estimates (useful lives, warranty costs, bad debts).

## Balance Sheet Elements

Assets: Probable future economic benefits controlled by a business entity as a result of a past transaction or event; resources that can be either sold for cash or used in operations.

Current Assets: Assets that can be converted into cash, sold, or consumed within one year or one operating cycle (whichever is longer).

Cash: Currency, coin, demand deposits, and any other instrument readily acceptable by a bank for deposit.

Cash Equivalents: Short-term, highly liquid investments that are (a) readily convertible into known amounts of cash and (b) so near maturity that they present insignificant risk of changes in value because of changes in interest rates. Generally, only investments with original maturities of three months or less to the holder qualify (e.g., U.S. Treasury Bills, commercial paper, money market mutual funds).

Accounts Receivable: Amounts owed to a company by customers from credit sales of goods or services in the ordinary course of business.

Notes Receivable: Written promises to pay a sum of money (usually with interest) at a specified future date; can be current or noncurrent.

Merchandise Inventory: Goods purchased or produced for sale to customers in the ordinary course of business.

Prepaid Expenses: Costs paid in advance for benefits to be received in future periods (e.g., rent, insurance, advertising); reported as current assets until consumed.

Noncurrent (Long-Term) Assets: Assets not expected to be converted to cash or consumed within one year or one operating cycle.

Long-Term Investments: Marketable securities and other investments that the company does NOT expect to dispose of in the short run.

Property, Plant and Equipment (PP&E): Tangible long-lived assets used in the company's operations that are expected to benefit the business for many years (e.g., land, buildings, machinery, equipment).

Intangible Assets: Assets that lack physical substance but are expected to benefit the company's operations for many years (e.g., patents, trademarks, copyrights, goodwill).

Goodwill: The intangible asset recognized when a company acquires another business for more than the fair value of the acquired identifiable net assets; not amortized but tested at least annually for impairment.

Liabilities: Obligations or debts that an entity must pay in money or services at some time in the future because of past transactions or events; accountants focus on the "probable sacrifice of future economic benefits."

Current Liabilities: Obligations that are reasonably expected to be paid using current assets or by creating other current liabilities, typically within 12 months or one operating cycle, whichever is longer.

Accounts Payable: Short-term obligations owed by a company to its suppliers for goods or services purchased on credit.

Unearned Revenue (Deferred Revenue): A liability created when a company accepts payment in advance for goods or services it will deliver in the future (e.g., customer deposits, unredeemed gift cards).

Short-Term Notes Payable: Debt payable to banks or other lenders within the next 12 months.

Current Portion of Long-Term Debt: The principal portion of long-term debt that is due to be paid within one year of the balance sheet date.

Noncurrent (Long-Term) Liabilities: Obligations that are not expected to be settled within one year or one operating cycle.

Long-Term Debt: Amounts borrowed from creditors that are scheduled to be repaid more than one year in the future (e.g., notes payable, mortgages payable, bonds payable).

Pension Liability: The estimated obligation to provide future pension benefits to employees for services already rendered.

Deferred Tax Liability: An obligation to pay income taxes in future periods, arising from temporary differences between the carrying amounts of assets and liabilities for financial reporting and their tax bases.

Stockholders' (Owners') Equity: The residual interest in the assets of the entity after deducting liabilities; represents the owners' claim on the business.

Capital Stock: The shares issued by the company, including common stock and preferred stock; represents direct ownership investment.

Common Stock: The basic ownership share of a corporation, typically carrying voting rights and a residual claim on assets and earnings.

Preferred Stock: A class of equity that carries certain preferences over common stock (often a fixed dividend and a priority claim in liquidation) but usually lacks voting rights.

Additional Paid-In Capital: Amounts contributed by stockholders for capital stock in excess of the stock's par or stated value.

Retained Earnings: The cumulative total net income earned by a company during its economic life, minus any amounts paid out as dividends to stockholders during that same period. Ending RE = Beginning RE + Net Income – Dividends Declared.

Dividends: Distributions of earnings (typically cash) made by a corporation to its stockholders; declared by the board of directors.

Treasury Stock: A corporation's own previously issued shares that have been reacquired but not retired; reported as a reduction of stockholders' equity.

## Accrual Concepts

Accrual Basis Accounting: A method under which revenues are recognized in the period in which the performance obligation to the customer is satisfied, and expenses are recognized in the period in which they are incurred (helped generate a benefit), regardless of when cash is received or paid.

Cash Basis Accounting: A method under which revenues increase net income when cash is received from customers and expenses decrease net income when cash is paid; does not require adjusting entries to accrue or defer revenues and expenses. Not in conformity with GAAP for most businesses.

Revenue Recognition Principle: The accrual-basis rule that revenue is recognized when (or as) an entity satisfies its performance obligation to a customer—generally when control of the promised goods or services is transferred.

Performance Obligation: A promise in a contract with a customer to transfer a distinct good or service; revenue is recognized when (or as) the obligation is satisfied.

Matching Principle: The rule that expenses (effort) should be recognized in the same period as the revenues (accomplishment) they helped to generate, so that expenses appear on the income statement in the period in which they benefited the company, not necessarily when cash was paid.

Earnings Process: The sequence of activities through which a company creates and delivers value—typically start production, complete production, deliver the product or service (often the trigger for revenue recognition), collect cash, and resolve post-delivery obligations such as warranties.

Adjusting Entries: Journal entries recorded at the end of an accounting period to update accounts so that revenues are recognized when earned and expenses when incurred, in accordance with the accrual basis.

Accrued Revenue: Revenue that has been earned but not yet received in cash or recorded (recognized through an adjusting entry that increases a receivable and revenue).

Accrued Expense: An expense that has been incurred but not yet paid in cash or recorded (recognized through an adjusting entry that increases an expense and a payable, e.g., wages payable).

Deferred Revenue: Cash received in advance of earning the related revenue; recorded initially as a liability and reduced as the company satisfies its performance obligation.

Deferred Expense (Prepaid Expense): Cash paid in advance of incurring the related expense; recorded initially as an asset and expensed over the periods benefited.

## Statement of Cash Flows

Operating Activities: Cash flows from the company's transactions and events that relate to its operations—inflows from selling goods and services, and outflows for operating costs such as salaries, advertising, and insurance.

Investing Activities: Cash flows arising from activities involving non-current assets and equity or debt investments in outside entities—inflows from the sale of property, equipment, and investment securities, and outflows for their purchase.

Financing Activities: Cash flows that provide the entity with resources from either its owners or creditors—inflows from issuing stock or debt, and outflows for repayment of principal on debt, payment of dividends, and stock repurchases.

Direct Method: A presentation of cash flow from operating activities that reports the major classes of gross cash receipts and gross cash payments (e.g., cash collected from customers, cash paid to suppliers).

Indirect Method: A presentation of cash flow from operating activities that starts with accrual-basis net income and adjusts for (a) non-cash items affecting net income (e.g., depreciation expense) and (b) changes in operating-related current assets and current liabilities. Increases in current assets are subtracted (and decreases added); increases in current liabilities are added (and decreases subtracted); the change in Cash itself is ignored.

Operating Cycle: The average length of time it takes a business to convert cash into inventory, inventory into accounts receivable, and accounts receivable back into cash.

## Internal Controls, Cash, and Ethics

Internal Control System: The policies and procedures designed by management to ensure reliable financial reporting, effective and efficient operations, compliance with applicable laws and regulations, and the safeguarding of assets against theft or unauthorized use.

COSO Framework: The framework developed by the Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission, recognized as the leading framework for designing, implementing, and conducting internal control systems and assessing their effectiveness. Its five components are:
1. Control Environment — the integrity and ethical values of the company;
2. Risk Assessment — management's identification of potential risks;
3. Control Activities — the specific internal controls implemented;
4. Information and Communication — the internal and external reporting processes; and
5. Monitoring — the over-time assessment and correction of internal controls.

Control Environment: The "tone at the top," encompassing integrity, ethical values, management's philosophy and operating style, organizational structure, and the assignment of authority and responsibility.

Risk Assessment: The process by which management identifies and analyzes risks that could prevent the achievement of the entity's objectives.

Control Activities: Specific policies and procedures—such as authorizations, reconciliations, performance reviews, segregation of duties, and physical safeguards—designed to mitigate identified risks.

Segregation of Duties: A fundamental internal control concept requiring that the responsibilities for authorizing transactions, recording transactions, and maintaining custody of related assets be assigned to different people, so that no single individual can both perpetrate and conceal errors or fraud.

Information and Communication: The internal and external reporting processes that capture and communicate relevant, quality information needed to fulfill internal control responsibilities.

Monitoring: Ongoing and separate evaluations of the effectiveness of internal controls over time, and the timely communication and correction of identified deficiencies.

Bank Reconciliation: A summary, usually prepared monthly, comparing the cash balance per the bank statement with the cash balance per the depositor's books to identify and explain differences and arrive at the true cash balance.

Deposits in Transit: Deposits made by the depositor and recorded on the books that have not yet been processed by the bank as of the statement date; added to the bank balance in a reconciliation.

Outstanding Checks: Checks written by the depositor and recorded on the books that have not yet cleared the bank by the statement date; deducted from the bank balance in a reconciliation.

Bank Service Charges: Monthly fees charged by the bank that appear on the bank statement but not yet on the depositor's books; deducted from the book balance in a reconciliation.

Collection of Receivables by the Bank: An amount owed to the depositor (e.g., from a customer's note) that was paid directly to the bank by a third party and added to the depositor's account; added to the book balance in a reconciliation.

NSF (Non-Sufficient Funds) Check: A customer's check that the bank could not collect because of insufficient funds in the customer's account; deducted from the book balance because the company must reinstate the receivable.

Petty Cash Fund: A small amount of cash kept on hand to pay for minor expenditures, controlled through an imprest system in which the fund is periodically replenished to its original balance.

Ethics: The system of moral principles and standards of conduct that guide professional accounting behavior, including integrity, objectivity, independence, due care, and confidentiality.

## Receivables and Bad Debts

Accounts Receivable (Trade Receivables): Amounts owed by customers from credit sales arising in the ordinary course of business.

Net Realizable Value: The amount expected to be collected in cash; for accounts receivable, it equals Accounts Receivable minus the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts.

Allowance for Doubtful Accounts: A contra-asset account that reduces gross Accounts Receivable to the amount estimated to be collectible (i.e., to net realizable value).

Bad Debts Expense (Uncollectible Accounts Expense): The expense reflecting estimated uncollectible accounts; recognized on the income statement in the period in which the related credit sales occur, consistent with the matching principle.

Allowance Method: A GAAP-compliant approach in which the company estimates uncollectible accounts at the end of each period and records the estimate as Bad Debts Expense with an offsetting credit to the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts; required when uncollectible amounts are material.

Allowance Method – Percentage of Sales (Income Statement Approach): An estimation technique under the allowance method in which Bad Debts Expense is computed by applying a historical percentage to credit sales for the period; the income statement (Sales) drives the income statement account (Bad Debts Expense).

Allowance Method – Percentage of Receivables (Balance Sheet Approach): An estimation technique under the allowance method in which the desired ending balance in the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts is computed by applying a percentage to the ending accounts receivable balance; the adjustment to Bad Debts Expense is whatever amount brings the Allowance account to that desired balance.

Aging of Accounts Receivable: A refinement of the percentage-of-receivables approach in which receivables are stratified by the length of time outstanding and progressively higher uncollectibility rates are applied to older categories.

Direct Write-Off Method: A method that recognizes Bad Debts Expense only when a specific account is identified as uncollectible. It violates the matching principle and is not in conformity with GAAP for material amounts, but is permitted for tax reporting in the United States.

Account Receivable Write-Off: The journal entry that removes a specific uncollectible account—debiting Allowance for Doubtful Accounts and crediting Accounts Receivable. Under the allowance method, the write-off does not reduce net realizable value or net income.

Recovery of a Written-Off Account: The reinstatement of a previously written-off receivable when the customer ultimately pays; involves reversing the original write-off and then recording the cash collection.

## Financial Statement Analysis

Financial Statement Analysis: The examination of the relationships among financial statement numbers (financial ratios) and of the trends in those numbers over time; it is both diagnostic (assessing past and current condition) and prognostic (predicting future performance).

Financial Ratios: Mathematical expressions describing the relationship between two financial statement numbers; useful for comparing companies of different sizes and for tracking performance over time.

Horizontal Analysis: A technique that compares each financial statement line item across multiple periods to identify trends and growth rates.

Vertical (Common-Size) Analysis: A technique that expresses each line item of a financial statement as a percentage of a base amount (e.g., each income statement item as a percentage of net sales, each balance sheet item as a percentage of total assets) to facilitate cross-company comparison.

Liquidity: A company's ability to meet its short-term obligations as they come due.

Working Capital: An absolute measure of liquidity equal to Current Assets minus Current Liabilities.

Current Ratio: A relative measure of liquidity computed as Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities. Historically, a ratio below 2:1 was considered indicative of liquidity concerns; today many companies operate effectively at ratios below 1:1.

Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio): A more rigorous test of liquidity that excludes inventory and prepaid expenses, focusing on the most liquid of a company's current assets: (Cash + Short-Term Investments + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities.

Solvency: A company's ability to meet its long-term obligations and remain financially viable in the long run.

Debt Ratio: The percentage of total funds a company has acquired through borrowing, computed as Total Liabilities ÷ Total Assets. Larger ratios indicate greater reliance on debt financing and higher perceived insolvency risk.

Debt-to-Equity Ratio: The number of dollars of borrowing for each dollar of equity investment, computed as Total Liabilities ÷ Total Equity. Larger ratios indicate higher financial leverage and risk; smaller ratios indicate greater reliance on equity financing and lower risk.

Profitability: A company's ability to generate earnings relative to its sales, assets, or equity.

Return on Sales (Profit Margin): The percentage of each sales dollar that becomes net income, computed as Net Income ÷ Net Sales; measures profitability.

Asset Turnover: A productivity measure computed as Net Sales ÷ Average Total Assets; indicates how efficiently a company uses its assets to generate sales.

Return on Investment (ROI) / Return on Assets (ROA): The amount of net income generated for each dollar of assets, computed as Net Income ÷ Average Total Assets. ROI can be decomposed into its two components: Return on Sales (profitability) × Asset Turnover (productivity).

Return on Equity (ROE): The amount of net income generated for each dollar of stockholders' equity, computed as Net Income ÷ Average Stockholders' Equity; provides an indication of a company's ability to generate a return for its owners.

DuPont Analysis: A framework that decomposes Return on Equity into Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier, isolating the operating, investing, and financing drivers of shareholder return.

Inventory Turnover: The number of times inventory is sold and replaced during a period, computed as Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Average Inventory; a measure of inventory management efficiency.

Days' Sales in Inventory: 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover; the average number of days inventory is held before being sold.

Accounts Receivable Turnover: Net Credit Sales ÷ Average Accounts Receivable; a measure of how quickly a company collects its receivables.

Days' Sales in Receivables (Average Collection Period): 365 ÷ Accounts Receivable Turnover; the average number of days it takes to collect a receivable.

Times Interest Earned: A solvency ratio computed as (Net Income + Interest Expense + Income Tax Expense) ÷ Interest Expense; indicates how many times operating earnings cover interest obligations.
