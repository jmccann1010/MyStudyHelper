## Module 1: Foundations of Financial Reporting

Financial Accounting: The process of translating a company's diverse economic activities into a standardized set of objective, quantitative measures — the financial statements — that communicate performance, financial position, and prospects to parties outside the firm.

Objectives of External Financial Reporting: The FASB's Conceptual Framework identifies the core purposes of financial reporting as providing information useful to investors and creditors for (1) assessing future cash flows, (2) evaluating financial health, (3) evaluating performance (earnings), and (4) understanding the entity's cash flows.

Economics of Accounting Information: The theoretical rationale for financial reporting — investors demand it because it reduces the information asymmetry and uncertainty inherent in evaluating investment opportunities, while companies supply it voluntarily (and are required to by regulation) because credible disclosure lowers their cost of capital by reducing the risk premium investors demand.

Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC): The U.S. federal agency with statutory authority to regulate companies whose securities trade on U.S. exchanges. The SEC's mandate is to ensure investors receive full and fair disclosure; it requires periodic filings including the Form 10-K (annual report), Form 10-Q (quarterly report), and Form 8-K (current report of material events).

Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB): The independent, private-sector body designated by the SEC to promulgate Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) in the United States through an open due-process standard-setting procedure (codified in the FASB Accounting Standards Codification, or ASC).

Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP): The authoritative body of accounting standards, conventions, and rules that U.S. companies must follow when preparing financial statements for external users, promulgated primarily by the FASB and codified in the ASC.

Income Statement: The financial statement reporting a firm's operating performance over a period of time (flow statement), summarizing revenues, expenses, gains, and losses to arrive at net income.

Accrual Basis Accounting: The GAAP-required basis of accounting under which revenues are recognized when the performance obligation to the customer is satisfied (not necessarily when cash is received) and expenses are recognized in the period they help generate benefit (not necessarily when cash is paid) — contrasted with cash basis accounting.

Revenue Recognition Principle: Under accrual accounting (and codified under ASC 606), revenue is recognized in the period in which the performance obligation to the customer is satisfied, regardless of when cash changes hands.

Matching Principle: The principle that expenses (effort) should be recognized in the same period as the revenues (accomplishment) they helped generate, aligning cost recognition with the economic benefit received rather than the timing of cash disbursement.

Revenues: Inflows of net assets (or settlements of liabilities) arising from an entity's ongoing major or central operations — delivering goods, rendering services, or other activities constituting the entity's core business — representing the "accomplishment" side of the earnings process.

Expenses: Outflows or consumption of assets incurred in generating revenue — the "effort" side of the earnings process — recognized in the period benefited rather than the period paid.

Gains and Losses: Increases or decreases in net assets from peripheral or incidental transactions, distinguished from revenues and expenses, which arise from an entity's central, ongoing operations.

Earnings Process: The economic sequence underlying revenue recognition — starting production, completing production, delivering the good/service, collecting cash, and (if applicable) the expiration of any related warranty obligation.

Net Income: The comprehensive "bottom line" performance measure computed as Revenues − Expenses + Gains − Losses, reflecting the overall economic performance of the entity for the period.

Gross Profit (Gross Margin): Net Sales minus Cost of Goods Sold; measures the profitability of a company's core product/service delivery before considering operating expenses.

Operating Income: Gross Profit minus Operating Expenses; reflects the profitability of the entity's core operating activities before financing costs, taxes, and non-operating items.

Operating vs. Nonoperating Items: Operating items (cost of goods sold, selling expenses, depreciation, R&D) relate to a company's principal, ongoing business activities. Nonoperating items (interest expense, interest/dividend income, gains/losses on securities) relate to financing and investing activities peripheral to the core operations.

Earnings Per Share (EPS): Net income attributable to common shareholders divided by the weighted-average number of common shares outstanding (on an equivalent-share basis). Basic EPS uses actual historical share counts; Diluted EPS incorporates the potential dilutive effect of convertible securities, options, and warrants, presenting a conservative (worst-case) view of per-share earnings.

Balance Sheet: The financial statement reporting a company's resources (assets) and the claims against those resources (liabilities and equity) at a specific point in time — a financial "snapshot," structured around the Accounting Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

Accounting Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity; the fundamental identity underlying double-entry bookkeeping, reflecting that a firm's uses of funds (assets) must equal its sources of funds (creditor and owner claims).

Assets: Probable future economic benefits controlled by an entity as a result of a past transaction or event, from which the firm can benefit either by converting them to cash or by using them in operations.

Historical Cost Concept: The GAAP convention that most assets are recorded and carried at the amount originally paid to acquire them, regardless of subsequent changes in market value (subject to exceptions such as impairment and fair-value measurement for certain financial instruments).

Current Assets: Assets expected to be converted to cash or consumed within one year or the operating cycle, whichever is longer; typically presented on the balance sheet in order of liquidity.

Operating Cycle: The average length of time required for a business to convert cash into inventory, inventory into receivables (via sale), and receivables back into cash.

Cash and Cash Equivalents: The most liquid current asset category, comprising currency, demand deposits, and short-term, highly liquid investments (see Cash Equivalents below).

Short-Term Investments: Marketable securities and other investments management intends to dispose of within the short run (generally one year).

Accounts Receivable, Net: Amounts due from customers for goods/services sold on credit, reported net of the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts to reflect estimated collectible value.

Merchandise Inventory: Goods purchased or manufactured for resale to customers, classified as a current asset until sold.

Prepaid Expenses: Costs paid in advance of consumption (e.g., rent, insurance, advertising) that represent a future economic benefit and are carried as current assets until the benefit is consumed.

Noncurrent Assets: Assets not expected to be converted to cash or consumed within one year or the operating cycle, including long-term investments, property/plant/equipment, and intangible assets.

Long-Term Investments: Marketable securities and other investments a company does not intend to dispose of in the short run.

Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E): Tangible, long-lived assets used in company operations (land, buildings, machinery, equipment, furniture) expected to provide benefit over multiple years.

Intangible Assets: Assets lacking physical substance (e.g., patents, trademarks, goodwill) expected to benefit operations over multiple years.

Liabilities: Probable future sacrifices of economic benefits arising from present obligations to transfer assets or provide services to other entities as a result of past transactions or events.

Current Liabilities: Obligations reasonably expected to be settled using current assets or by creating other current liabilities within one year or the operating cycle.

Accounts Payable: Amounts owed to suppliers for goods and services purchased on credit in the ordinary course of business.

Accrued Liabilities (Accrued Expenses): Obligations for expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid in cash (e.g., wages payable, interest payable, income taxes payable) — a direct product of accrual accounting.

Unearned Revenue (Deferred Revenue): A liability arising when a company receives payment in advance of delivering the related goods or services (e.g., customer deposits, gift cards, subscription prepayments); recognized as revenue only as the performance obligation is satisfied.

Short-Term Notes Payable: Formal, interest-bearing debt owed to banks or other lenders due within the next 12 months.

Current Portion of Long-Term Debt: The principal portion of long-term debt scheduled to be repaid within the next 12 months; reclassified out of long-term debt to current liabilities.

Noncurrent Liabilities: Obligations not due within one year or the operating cycle, including long-term debt (notes, mortgages, bonds payable) and other long-term liabilities such as pension and deferred tax liabilities.

Stockholders' Equity: The residual interest of owners in the assets of a company after deducting liabilities; principal components include capital stock (common and preferred), additional paid-in capital, and retained earnings.

Book Value: Total Assets minus Total Liabilities (i.e., Stockholders' Equity), representing the accounting (historical-cost-based) measure of owners' claims on the firm.

Market Value (Market Capitalization): The market price per share multiplied by the number of shares outstanding; frequently diverges materially from book value because it reflects the market's forward-looking assessment of future cash flows rather than historical cost.

Statement of Cash Flows: The financial statement reporting cash inflows and outflows classified into operating, investing, and financing activities during a period, reconciling the change in the cash balance.

Operating Activities (Cash Flow): Cash flows arising from transactions that enter into the determination of net income — the production and delivery of goods/services (e.g., collections from customers, payments for salaries, inventory, and other operating costs).

Investing Activities (Cash Flow): Cash flows from the acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and investment securities (e.g., purchases/sales of PP&E and investment securities).

Financing Activities (Cash Flow): Cash flows associated with obtaining resources from owners (equity issuances, dividend payments) and creditors (debt issuances, principal repayments).

Retained Earnings: The cumulative net income earned by a company over its life, less cumulative dividends distributed to shareholders. Roll-forward: Beginning Retained Earnings + Net Income − Dividends = Ending Retained Earnings.

Management Discussion and Analysis (MD&A): A required narrative section of SEC filings in which management interprets recent financial performance, discusses forward-looking expectations, and explains critical accounting policies and estimates — intended to give investors management's candid perspective.

Independent Auditor's Report: The auditor's formal opinion on whether the financial statements are presented fairly, in all material respects, in conformity with GAAP. The audit provides reasonable (not absolute) assurance based on sampling of transactions, not a guarantee against all misstatement, and the financial statements themselves remain management's responsibility.

Financial Statement Footnotes: Disclosures accompanying the primary financial statements that detail the assumptions, estimates, measurement policies, and additional context underlying the summary numbers reported in the statements — an integral part of a GAAP-compliant financial statement package.

## Module 2: Financial Statement Analysis, Internal Controls, and Cash

### Liquidity Analysis

Financial Statement Analysis: The examination of relationships among financial statement figures (ratio analysis) and trends in those figures over time, used to assess a company's liquidity, solvency, and profitability.

Working Capital: Current Assets − Current Liabilities; an absolute-dollar measure of short-run debt-paying ability.

Current Ratio: Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities; a relative measure of short-term liquidity. Historically, ratios below 2:1 signaled potential liquidity concern, though ratios below 1:1 have become common in modern retail/service industries.

Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio): (Cash + Short-Term Investments + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities; a more conservative liquidity measure than the current ratio because it excludes inventory and prepaid items, which are less readily convertible to cash.

### Solvency Analysis

Debt Ratio: Total Liabilities ÷ Total Assets; measures the percentage of a company's assets financed through borrowing. A higher ratio signals greater reliance on debt and greater insolvency risk, all else equal.

Debt-to-Equity Ratio: Total Liabilities ÷ Total Equity; measures dollars of debt financing per dollar of equity financing. Interpretation mirrors the debt ratio — higher values indicate greater leverage and risk.

### Profitability Analysis

Return on Investment (ROI) / Return on Assets: Net Income ÷ Average Total Assets; measures overall performance relative to the asset base employed.

DuPont Decomposition of ROI: ROI = Profit Margin × Asset Turnover, where Profit Margin = Net Income ÷ Net Sales (measuring profitability per sales dollar) and Asset Turnover = Net Sales ÷ Average Assets (measuring how productively assets generate sales). This decomposition isolates whether ROI is driven by margin efficiency or asset utilization.

Return on Equity (ROE): Net Income ÷ Average Stockholders' Equity; measures the return generated for the residual (owner) claimants on the business, and is often higher than ROI due to financial leverage.

### Internal Control and Governance

Internal Control System: The policies and procedures an organization designs to provide reasonable assurance regarding reliable financial reporting, effective and efficient operations, compliance with laws and regulations, and safeguarding of assets against theft or unauthorized use.

Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX, 2002): Federal legislation enacted in response to major accounting scandals (e.g., Enron, WorldCom). Section 404 places responsibility on management for establishing and maintaining an effective internal control structure over financial reporting and requires public companies to annually assess and report on the effectiveness of those controls.

COSO Framework: The internal control framework issued by the Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission, widely recognized as the leading model for designing, implementing, and assessing internal control systems. Its five interrelated components are:

- *Control Environment* — the integrity and ethical values that set the tone of an organization.

- *Risk Assessment* — management's identification and analysis of risks to achieving objectives.

- *Control Activities* — the specific policies and procedures (internal controls) that mitigate identified risks.

- *Information and Communication* — the internal/external reporting processes supporting control functioning.

- *Monitoring* — ongoing evaluation to ensure controls remain effective over time.

### Cash

Cash Equivalents: Short-term, highly liquid investments (e.g., U.S. Treasury bills, commercial paper, money market funds) that are readily convertible to known amounts of cash and so near maturity (generally original maturities of three months or less) that they present insignificant risk of value change from interest rate fluctuations.

Bank Reconciliation: The process of reconciling the bank's reported cash balance with the company's book (general ledger) cash balance, identifying and adjusting for timing differences and errors so both balances arrive at the true, correct cash figure.

Deposit in Transit: A deposit recorded on the company's books that has not yet cleared/posted at the bank as of the statement date — added to the bank balance in reconciliation.

Outstanding Checks: Checks written and recorded by the depositor that have not yet cleared the bank — subtracted from the bank balance in reconciliation.

NSF (Not-Sufficient-Funds) Check: A customer's check returned by the bank due to insufficient funds in the customer's account; deducted from the book (general ledger) balance during reconciliation since the expected cash inflow did not materialize.

Bank Service Charges: Fees charged by the bank (e.g., monthly maintenance fees) that reduce the book cash balance and must be recorded by the depositor upon discovery during reconciliation.

## Module 3: Operating Cash Flow and Accounts Receivable

Indirect Method (Operating Cash Flow): The most common approach to presenting cash flows from operating activities, beginning with accrual-basis net income and adjusting for (1) non-cash items affecting net income (e.g., depreciation expense) and (2) changes in operating-related current assets and current liabilities, effectively converting accrual-basis income to a cash-basis measure.

Reconciling Net Income to Operating Cash Flow — Adjustment Rules:

- An *increase* in an operating current asset is *subtracted*; a *decrease* is *added* (an increase in receivables, for example, means revenue was recognized but cash was not yet collected).

- An *increase* in an operating current liability is *added*; a *decrease* is *subtracted* (an increase in payables means an expense was recognized but cash was not yet paid).

Cash Basis vs. Accrual Basis Income Measurement: Under cash-basis accounting, revenues increase net income only when cash is received, and expenses decrease net income only when cash is paid — no adjusting entries are needed to accrue/defer revenue and expense recognition, unlike accrual accounting.

Accounts Receivable: Amounts owed to a business by its customers as a result of having delivered goods or rendered services for which cash has not yet been collected; reported on the balance sheet at net realizable value.

Net Realizable Value: The amount of a receivable expected to actually be collected in cash, computed as gross Accounts Receivable less the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts.

Allowance Method (for Uncollectible Accounts): The GAAP-required method (consistent with the matching principle) for recognizing bad debt expense in the period of the related credit sale, via an estimate, rather than waiting until a specific account is known to be uncollectible (the non-GAAP direct write-off method).

Percent of Receivables Method (Balance Sheet Approach): An allowance-method estimation technique focused on determining the desired ending balance in the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts, based on a percentage of the year-end (or aged) accounts receivable balance. Bad Debts Expense is the "plug" needed to bring the Allowance account to its desired balance.

Percent of Sales (Revenue) Method (Income Statement Approach): An allowance-method estimation technique that computes Bad Debts Expense directly as a percentage of period credit sales; the Allowance account balance is a byproduct rather than the target of the estimate.

Write-Off of an Uncollectible Account: The formal removal of a specific customer's balance from Accounts Receivable and the corresponding reduction of the Allowance for Doubtful Accounts by the same amount. Critically, a write-off does not change net realizable value or affect income, since both the gross receivable and its associated allowance are reduced by the identical amount.

Aged Accounts Receivable Schedule: A listing of outstanding receivables categorized by the length of time they have been outstanding, often used to apply differentiated uncollectibility percentages under the balance sheet (percent-of-receivables) approach.

## Module 4: Notes Receivable and Receivables Analysis

Promissory Note: A written, unconditional promise to pay a specified amount (principal plus interest) to another party at a specified future (maturity) date.

Note Receivable: A formal, interest-bearing claim against a debtor, evidenced by a promissory note, distinguished from a routine (informal, non-interest-bearing) account receivable.

Simple Interest Formula: Interest = Principal × Rate × Time (P × r × t), where Time is expressed as a fraction of a year; used to compute both accrued interest at a balance sheet date and total interest at maturity.

Interest Receivable: An asset representing interest earned but not yet collected, accrued at the end of an accounting period in accordance with the matching/accrual principle even though the cash will not be received until the note matures.

Accounts Receivable Turnover (ART): Net Credit Sales (or Total Revenue) ÷ Average (Gross) Accounts Receivable; measures how many times, on average, receivables are generated and collected during a period. A higher turnover indicates faster collection and greater liquidity of receivables.

Average Days to Collect Receivables (Days Sales Outstanding): 365 ÷ Accounts Receivable Turnover; the average number of days between a credit sale and its collection. A lower value is generally more favorable.

Insights from Declining Receivables Turnover: A slowing turnover rate may reflect genuine deterioration in customer collectability, but alternative explanations include relaxed/extended credit terms or a deliberate strategic shift toward longer-paying (but perhaps larger or more creditworthy) customers. Management responses can include tightening credit-granting scrutiny or offering early-payment discounts (e.g., moving credit terms from 1/10, n/30 to 2/10, n/30) to incentivize faster collection.

## Module 5: Inventory and Cost of Goods Sold

Cost of Goods Sold (CGS/COGS): Beginning Inventory + Purchases − Ending Inventory = Cost of Goods Sold; represents the cost of inventory transferred from the balance sheet to the income statement upon sale, matched against sales revenue to determine gross profit.

Cost of Goods Available for Sale (CGAS): Beginning Inventory + Purchases during the period; the total pool of inventory cost that must be allocated between what remains on hand (ending inventory) and what was sold (COGS).

Physical Flow vs. Cost Flow Assumption: *Physical flow* is the actual physical movement of goods in and out of a business (e.g., a grocer typically sells oldest stock first). The *cost flow assumption* is a distinct accounting convention chosen for financial reporting purposes to allocate costs between inventory and COGS, and need not mirror the actual physical flow of goods.

First-In, First-Out (FIFO): An inventory costing method assuming the oldest (first-acquired) costs are the first transferred to COGS upon sale, leaving the most recently incurred costs in ending inventory. In a period of rising prices, FIFO produces the lowest COGS, the highest ending inventory value, and the highest reported net income (and tax liability) relative to LIFO.

Last-In, First-Out (LIFO): An inventory costing method assuming the most recently incurred costs are the first transferred to COGS upon sale, leaving the oldest costs in ending inventory. In a period of rising prices, LIFO produces the highest COGS, the lowest ending inventory value, and the lowest reported net income (and tax liability) relative to FIFO — a key reason some U.S. companies elect LIFO (note: IFRS prohibits LIFO).

Weighted-Average Cost Method: An inventory costing method assuming units in ending inventory are representative of the full pool of units available for sale. Weighted-Average Cost per Unit = Cost of Goods Available for Sale ÷ Total Units Available for Sale; this single unit cost is applied to both ending inventory and COGS.

Inventory Turnover: Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Average Inventory; measures how many times, on average, inventory is sold and replaced during a period. A higher turnover indicates faster-moving, more liquid inventory; a persistently low turnover can signal excess or obsolete inventory.

Average Days to Sell Inventory: 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover; the average number of days inventory is held before sale. A lower value indicates more efficient inventory management.

Manufacturing Costs — Direct Materials: The physical ingredients/components that are directly converted into a saleable finished product.

Manufacturing Costs — Direct Labor: Wages of production employees directly engaged in converting raw materials into the finished, saleable product.

Manufacturing Overhead: Indirect factory-related costs not directly traceable to specific units of product (e.g., plant depreciation, factory utilities, plant supervisory salaries).

Raw Materials Inventory: Goods acquired in an unfinished state that will eventually become part of a finished product (a manufacturing-firm inventory category).

Work-in-Process Inventory: Partially completed products that have entered but not yet completed the manufacturing process.

Finished Goods Inventory: Fully completed products awaiting sale to customers.

## Module 6: Property, Plant & Equipment

Property, Plant, and Equipment (PP&E): Tangible, long-lived assets acquired for use in business operations rather than for resale, including land, buildings, machinery, equipment, furniture, leasehold improvements, and construction in progress.

Leasehold Improvements: Costs incurred by a lessee (tenant) to customize leased property for the lessee's own operational use; capitalized and amortized (typically over the shorter of the lease term or the improvement's useful life).

Construction in Progress: An accumulation account capturing the costs of constructing a long-term asset (e.g., a building) while under construction; once the asset is placed into service, its accumulated cost is reclassified out of Construction in Progress and into the appropriate asset category (e.g., Buildings).

Acquisition Cost (PP&E): Under the historical cost principle, PP&E is recorded at all costs necessary to bring the asset to the condition and location required for its intended use — for land, this includes purchase price, sales tax, title/transfer costs, realtor commissions, legal fees, surveying fees, and demolition costs of old structures; for equipment, it includes purchase price (net of discounts), sales tax, freight/delivery, installation, and costs to adapt the asset for its intended use.

Basket (Lump-Sum) Purchase: The acquisition of multiple assets for a single, combined purchase price, requiring the total price to be allocated among the individual assets in proportion to their relative fair (appraised) values, since each asset class (e.g., land, which is non-depreciable, vs. building, which is depreciable) must be recorded separately.

Depreciation: The systematic and rational allocation of a tangible long-lived asset's depreciable cost to the accounting periods benefited by its use — explicitly *not* a cash-fund accumulation mechanism and *not* a mechanism for tracking current market value.

Causes of Depreciation: *Physical deterioration* (wear from use, time, and exposure to elements) and *obsolescence* (the asset becoming outdated, outmoded, or inadequate for current needs), both of which diminish an asset's service potential over time.

Salvage Value (Residual Value): The estimated amount for which an asset can be sold (or is otherwise worth) at the end of its useful life; excluded from the amount subject to depreciation.

Depreciable Cost: Acquisition Cost minus Residual (Salvage) Value; the portion of an asset's cost that will be systematically expensed over its useful life.

Estimated Useful Life: Management's estimate of an asset's service potential, expressed in years (time-based methods) or units of output/activity (activity-based methods).

Straight-Line Depreciation Method: Annual Depreciation = (Cost − Residual Value) ÷ Useful Life; allocates an equal dollar amount of depreciation expense to each period of the asset's useful life. Appropriate for assets whose economic benefits diminish roughly uniformly over time, and the most commonly used method in practice due to its simplicity.

Double-Declining-Balance (DDB) Method: An accelerated depreciation method computing annual depreciation as (2 ÷ Useful Life) × Beginning-of-Year Book Value, ignoring residual value until the final year(s), when depreciation is limited so the asset is not depreciated below residual value. Appropriate for assets that deliver a disproportionate share of their economic benefit in early years of use.

Units-of-Production Method: An activity-based depreciation method computing a per-unit depreciation rate as (Cost − Residual Value) ÷ Estimated Total Output, then multiplying that rate by actual units of output/usage each period. Appropriate when an asset's decline in usefulness correlates directly with its level of use rather than the passage of time.

Depreciation and Cash Flow: Depreciation is a non-cash expense — it does not itself represent or use a source of cash. Its cash-flow relevance is indirect: by reducing taxable income, depreciation lowers the income taxes actually paid in cash, thereby providing a "tax shield" benefit.

Disposal of PP&E: Upon retirement, sale, or trade-in of a long-lived asset, depreciation must first be recorded up to the disposal date, after which any gain (proceeds exceed remaining book value) or loss (proceeds are less than remaining book value) is recognized, typically reported on the income statement below Operating Income (as a non-operating item).

Gain/Loss on Disposal: Computed as Proceeds Received minus Book Value at Disposal (Cost minus Accumulated Depreciation as of the disposal date). A positive difference is a gain; a negative difference is a loss.

Revenue Expenditure: A cost that does not benefit future accounting periods, or is immaterial in amount, and is therefore expensed immediately on the income statement (e.g., ordinary repairs and maintenance).

Capital Expenditure: A cost that benefits future accounting periods (extends useful life or increases productive capacity) and is therefore capitalized on the balance sheet as part of the related asset's cost, to be depreciated over the remaining useful life.

PP&E (Fixed Asset) Turnover: Net Sales ÷ Average PP&E, Net; measures how productively a company converts its investment in long-term operational assets into sales revenue — a higher ratio indicates more efficient asset utilization.

## Module 7: Liabilities — Notes, Contingencies, Warranties, and Long-Term Debt

Notes Payable: A formal, written, interest-bearing obligation to repay a specific principal amount at a specified future date, distinguished from an informal account payable by the existence of a promissory note and explicit interest terms.

Interest Payable: An accrued liability representing interest expense incurred but not yet paid in cash as of the balance sheet date, recorded via an adjusting entry consistent with accrual accounting.

Sales Tax Payable: A current liability representing sales tax collected from customers on behalf of a taxing authority (e.g., a state government), which the retailer holds temporarily before remitting; it is not revenue to the retailer.

Contingent Liability: A potential obligation whose existence and/or ultimate amount depends on the outcome of a future event (e.g., pending litigation). Under GAAP (ASC 450), a contingent liability is *recognized* on the balance sheet if it is both probable and the amount is reasonably estimable; if only one of these two criteria is met, the contingency is instead disclosed in the footnotes rather than recognized.

Certainty Continuum (for Contingencies): The conceptual spectrum from "impossible" to "certain" along which the likelihood of a contingent event is assessed, informing whether GAAP requires recognition, disclosure, or no action (probable, reasonably possible, or remote, respectively, under ASC 450 terminology).

Warranty: A seller's promise to provide free repair, replacement, or services on products that fail to perform satisfactorily within a specified period. Under the matching principle, Warranty (Estimated Warranty) Expense is estimated and accrued in the same period as the related sale, rather than when actual warranty claims are serviced, and a corresponding Warranty (Estimated Warranty) Liability is recorded.

Bonds Payable: A form of long-term debt financing in which a company (issuer) borrows a principal amount from a broad pool of investors (bondholders) in exchange for periodic interest payments and repayment of the face value at maturity — typically the largest single category of noncurrent liability for large corporations.

Installment Notes Payable: A form of long-term debt repaid through a series of periodic (e.g., monthly) payments that include both an interest component and a principal-reduction component; the periodic interest is computed on the *outstanding* note balance (interest rate per compounding period × remaining principal), and each payment's principal reduction increases as the interest component shrinks over the note's life (i.e., an amortizing loan).

## Module 8: Stockholders' Equity

Contributed Capital: Proceeds received by a company from original stock issuances, net of amounts paid to repurchase shares from investors; one of the two principal components of total stockholders' equity (the other being earned capital).

Earned Capital: Retained Earnings plus Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income (AOCI); represents equity generated through operations and other comprehensive gains/losses, as opposed to capital contributed directly by shareholders.

Preferred Stock: A class of capital stock carrying preferences over common stock — typically a *dividend preference* (preferred dividends paid before any common dividends) and a *liquidation preference* (preferred claims settled before common claims in liquidation) — usually in exchange for giving up voting rights and, often, fixed (rather than variable/upside) dividend participation.

Common Stock: The class of capital stock representing the residual ownership interest in a corporation, typically carrying voting rights but subordinate claims to both dividends and liquidation proceeds relative to preferred stock.

Authorized Shares: The maximum number of shares of stock a corporation is permitted to issue under its articles of incorporation.

Issued Shares: The number of shares that have actually been sold or awarded to external parties (a subset of authorized shares).

Outstanding Shares: The number of issued shares currently held by external parties (excludes shares reacquired by the corporation as treasury stock).

Par Value: The minimum legal amount at which a corporation's stock can be issued, also referred to as minimum legal capital; largely a legal/historical construct with little economic significance for modern equity valuation.

Stated Value: A designated nominal value assigned to no-par stock, serving the same balance-sheet classification function as par value.

Additional Paid-In Capital (APIC): The amount of consideration received from investors in excess of a stock's par (or stated) value upon issuance; also referred to as "paid-in capital in excess of par."

Treasury Stock: Shares of a corporation's own stock that have been repurchased by the issuing corporation and are held with the intent of possible future reissuance. Treasury stock is recorded at cost as a *contra-equity account* (a reduction to total stockholders' equity), and carries neither voting nor dividend rights while held in treasury. Common motivations for buyback include funding employee stock option plans, increasing EPS, supporting the stock price, and defending against hostile takeovers.

Reissuance of Treasury Stock: Upon reissuing treasury shares, any excess of the reissuance price over the shares' original cost increases Additional Paid-In Capital (never income); GAAP prohibits recognizing a gain or loss on transactions in a company's own stock.

Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income (Loss) (AOCI): A stockholders' equity account accumulating other comprehensive income (OCI) items (e.g., unrealized gains/losses on certain securities, foreign currency translation adjustments, pension adjustments) that bypass the income statement but are reported on the Statement of Comprehensive Income; analogous to how Retained Earnings accumulates net income, AOCI accumulates OCI over time.

Cash Dividends: Distributions of cash from a corporation to its stockholders. While corporations are not obligated to declare dividends, once formally declared, a dividend becomes a legal liability (Dividends Payable) of the corporation.

Dividend Declaration, Record, and Payment Dates: The three key dates in the dividend process — the *declaration date* (when the board formally authorizes the dividend and a liability is recorded), the *date of record* (the date determining which shareholders are entitled to the dividend; no journal entry is made), and the *payment date* (when cash is actually disbursed and the liability is extinguished).

Cumulative Preferred Stock: Preferred stock under which any undeclared dividends accumulate as dividends in arrears and must be paid in full before any dividend can be paid to common shareholders in a future period.

Noncumulative Preferred Stock: Preferred stock under which undeclared dividends do not carry forward; if a dividend is skipped in a given year, preferred shareholders have no future claim to that missed amount.

Dividends in Arrears: Unpaid, accumulated dividends on cumulative preferred stock from prior periods, which must be satisfied before any common dividend can be declared; disclosed in the footnotes but not recognized as a formal balance-sheet liability until declared.

Stock Dividend: A distribution to shareholders in the form of additional shares of the company's own stock rather than cash. Because it is still classified as a dividend, Retained Earnings is reduced by the *market value* of the newly issued shares (with the offsetting entries increasing Common Stock at par and Additional Paid-in Capital for the excess) — a mechanism sometimes called "capitalizing" retained earnings. Total stockholders' equity is unchanged; only its internal composition shifts.

Stock Split: An increase in the number of shares authorized, issued, and outstanding accompanied by a proportionate decrease in par value per share (e.g., a 2-for-1 split doubles share count and halves par value). Unlike a stock dividend, a stock split requires no formal journal entry and does not change any dollar amount in stockholders' equity — only share count and per-share par value are affected.

## Module 9: Managerial Accounting Foundations and Product Costing

Product Cost: Costs directly related to production/manufacturing activities (direct materials, direct labor, and factory overhead); treated as an *asset* (Inventory) on the balance sheet until the related goods are sold, at which point the cost is transferred to the income statement as Cost of Goods Sold.

Period Cost: Costs not related to production activities (e.g., officer salaries, selling expenses, administrative expenses, interest expense); expensed on the income statement in the period incurred, regardless of when related sales occur.

Average Cost per Unit: Total (Product) Cost ÷ Number of Units Produced; the fundamental unit-costing calculation in manufacturing settings.

Upstream Costs: Costs incurred before the manufacturing process begins (e.g., research and development).

Midstream Costs: Costs incurred to manufacture the product itself — direct materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead.

Downstream Costs: Costs incurred after the manufacturing process is complete (e.g., advertising, distribution, customer service).

Managerial Accounting vs. Financial Accounting: Financial accounting is an information-processing system generating general-purpose reports for external users under mandatory GAAP standards, emphasizing objective, historical, financial data over long, standardized reporting periods. Managerial accounting is an information framework supporting internal decision-making, generating special-purpose reports (no external standards imposed) that may include nonfinancial measures (time, quality), are more detailed, are forward-looking, and are prepared as frequently as management needs require.

## Module 10: Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) Analysis and Cost Behavior

Contribution Margin: Sales Revenue minus Variable Expenses; the amount remaining from each sales dollar (or unit) available to cover fixed expenses and then contribute to profit.

Contribution Margin Ratio: Contribution Margin ÷ Sales Revenue; expresses contribution margin as a percentage of sales, useful for computing break-even and target-profit points in sales-dollar terms.

Contribution-Margin Income Statement: An internal-management-oriented income statement format that separates costs by behavior (variable vs. fixed) rather than by function (product vs. period), structured as Sales − Variable Expenses = Contribution Margin − Fixed Expenses = Net Income.

Break-Even Point: The volume of activity (in units or sales dollars) at which total revenues exactly equal total expenses, resulting in zero net income.

Break-Even Point (Unit Formula): Fixed Expenses ÷ Unit Contribution Margin = Break-even point in units.

Break-Even Point (Sales Dollar Formula): Fixed Expenses ÷ Contribution Margin Ratio = Break-even point in sales dollars.

Equation Approach (CVP): Sales Revenue − Variable Expenses − Fixed Expenses = Profit, expressed algebraically as (Unit Price × X) − (Unit Variable Cost × X) − Fixed Expenses = Target Profit, solved for the unknown volume X.

Target Net Profit Analysis: An extension of break-even analysis solving for the sales volume required to achieve a specified profit level: Units to Achieve Target Profit = (Fixed Expenses + Target Profit) ÷ Unit Contribution Margin.

Margin of Safety: Budgeted (or Actual) Sales minus Break-Even Sales; represents the amount by which sales can decline before the company begins to incur a loss — a measure of a company's cushion above break-even.

Cost Behavior: The way a cost responds to changes in the level of business activity; the foundational classification (variable, fixed, or mixed) underlying CVP analysis and cost estimation.

Variable Costs: Costs that change in total in direct proportion to changes in activity level, but remain constant on a per-unit basis (Total Variable Cost = b × X, where b is the variable cost per unit and X is activity volume).

Fixed Costs: Costs that do not change in total in response to changes in activity level (within the relevant range), but decrease on a per-unit basis as volume increases (Total Fixed Cost = a, a constant).

Mixed (Semi-Variable) Costs: Costs containing both a fixed and a variable component, increasing linearly with activity but positive even at zero activity (Total Mixed Cost = a + bX).

Relevant Range: The normal band of activity over which a company expects to operate, within which the assumed linear behavior of fixed and variable costs is expected to hold; outside this range, cost behavior assumptions may no longer be valid.

Cost Estimation: The process of determining the historical relationship between an activity driver and total cost, in order to forecast future costs; common techniques include the high-low method, scatter diagrams, and least-squares regression.

High-Low Method: A cost estimation technique using only the highest and lowest activity observations in a data set. Variable Cost per Unit = (Cost at High Activity − Cost at Low Activity) ÷ (High Activity Level − Low Activity Level); Total Fixed Cost is then derived by subtracting total variable cost from total cost at either the high or low point.

Scatter Diagram: A graphical plot of historical activity and cost observations (one dot per period), used to visually assess the nature of the cost relationship before or alongside more formal estimation techniques; relying on it alone requires subjective judgment.

Least-Squares Regression: A statistical technique that fits a cost-estimating equation to all observed data points by minimizing the sum of squared vertical deviations between actual and estimated costs at each observation — generally superior to the high-low method and scatter diagrams because it incorporates every data point rather than only two extremes, and it does not rely on subjective visual judgment.

Coefficient of Determination (R²): A statistical measure produced by regression analysis indicating the percentage of variation in the dependent variable (total cost) explained by variation in the independent variable (activity); used to assess how well a cost equation fits the underlying data.

Operating Leverage: A measure of the extent to which an organization's cost structure relies on fixed (versus variable) costs. Higher operating leverage means a given percentage change in sales revenue produces a proportionally larger percentage change in profit — magnifying both upside and downside outcomes.

Magnitude (Degree) of Operating Leverage: Contribution Margin ÷ Net Income; used to estimate the percentage change in net income resulting from a given percentage change in sales (% Δ Sales × Degree of Operating Leverage ≈ % Δ Net Income).

Risk and Reward Trade-Off in Cost Structure: Shifting a company's cost structure from fixed to variable reduces the potential for large losses when sales are low (lower fixed-cost risk exposure) but simultaneously reduces the potential for magnified profit gains when sales are high, since variable-cost structures do not produce operating leverage.
